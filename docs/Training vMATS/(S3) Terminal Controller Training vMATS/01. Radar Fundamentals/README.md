# Radar Fundamentals

## 1.1 General

Radar control is the provision of air traffic control service using surveillance systems to identify aircraft, monitor their position, and provide separation.

Approach control utilises radar to:
- Sequence arriving traffic efficiently
- Maintain separation between aircraft
- Provide vectoring to instrument approaches
- Manage traffic flow within the Terminal Manoeuvring Area (TMA)

---

## 1.2 Types of Surveillance

### 1.2.1 Primary Surveillance Radar (PSR)

Primary radar detects aircraft by transmitting radio waves and receiving reflected signals.

**Characteristics:**
- Does not require aircraft equipment
- Provides range and bearing only
- Does not provide identification or altitude

---

### 1.2.2 Secondary Surveillance Radar (SSR)

SSR operates through aircraft transponders and provides enhanced information.

**Modes:**
- **Mode A:** Squawk code (identification)
- **Mode C:** Pressure altitude
- **Mode S:** Aircraft-specific data (callsign, enhanced data where available)

**Advantages:**
- Positive identification capability
- Altitude readout
- Greater accuracy than PSR

---

## 1.3 Radar Identification

Before providing radar control, aircraft must be positively identified.

### 1.3.1 Methods of Identification

Controllers may identify aircraft using one of the following methods:

- **SSR Code Assignment**  
  Assigning a discrete squawk and observing it on the radar display

- **Squawk Ident**  
  Pilot activates IDENT, causing a temporary highlight on the radar display

- **Position Correlation**  
  Matching a radar return with a known aircraft position

- **Observed Manoeuvre**  
  Issuing a turn and observing the corresponding radar response

---

## 1.4 Radar Separation Standards

### 1.4.1 Lateral Separation

Minimum radar separation shall be:

- **3 NM** (training / VATSIM standard)  
- **5 NM** (real-world standard where applicable)

Controllers must ensure separation is maintained at all times.

---

### 1.4.2 Vertical Separation

- Minimum vertical separation: **1000 ft**

Controllers shall verify Mode C readout prior to applying vertical separation.

---

## 1.5 Controller Responsibilities

The radar controller shall:

- Maintain continuous surveillance of all aircraft under their control
- Ensure separation standards are not infringed
- Anticipate conflicts before they develop
- Issue clear, concise, and timely instructions
- Monitor pilot compliance with issued clearances

---

## 1.6 Limitations of Radar

Controllers must be aware of radar limitations, including:

- Radar coverage gaps
- Delayed or incorrect Mode C readouts
- Transponder failures
- Frequency congestion affecting communication

In such cases, alternative separation methods or increased spacing may be required.

---

## 1.7 Control Philosophy

Radar control is based on:

> **Anticipation, planning, and continuous monitoring**

Unlike aerodrome control, approach control requires:
- Managing multiple aircraft simultaneously
- Predicting aircraft trajectories
- Maintaining an orderly and efficient traffic flow

---

## 1.8 Safety Considerations

Controllers must prioritise:

- Separation over efficiency
- Stable approach profiles
- Early intervention in developing conflicts

At no time shall traffic flow be prioritised over safety.

---

## 1.9 Summary

Radar fundamentals form the foundation of approach control. Mastery of identification, separation, and surveillance principles is essential before progressing to vectoring and sequencing techniques.
