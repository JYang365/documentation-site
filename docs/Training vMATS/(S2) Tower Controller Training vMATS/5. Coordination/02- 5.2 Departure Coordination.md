# 5.2 Departure Coordination

Departure coordination ensures that aircraft departing the aerodrome are safely integrated into the en-route or approach environment. The Tower controller must obtain approval where required and ensure that departures do not conflict with arriving traffic.

---

## 5.2.1 Objectives

The controller aims to:

- Ensure safe integration of departures into controlled airspace  
- Prevent conflicts with arriving aircraft  
- Maintain efficient runway utilisation  
- Comply with coordination procedures  

---

## 5.2.2 When Coordination is Required

Coordination is required when:

- An Approach or Radar controller is online  
- Departures enter controlled airspace immediately after takeoff  
- Local procedures require a departure release  

If no higher unit is online, Tower assumes responsibility.

---

## 5.2.3 Departure Release

A departure release is approval from the next controlling unit to allow an aircraft to depart.

The Tower controller must:

- Request release before issuing takeoff clearance (if required)  
- Provide relevant information (callsign, runway, intended direction)  
- Wait for approval before proceeding  

Example coordination:

```text
TWR → APP: Request release, GFA123, runway 27, departure SID.
APP → TWR: GFA123 released.
APP → TWR: GFA123 released.
```

---

## 5.2.4 After Departure

After an aircraft has departed, the Tower controller must:

– Ensure the aircraft is transferred to the next unit at the correct time  
– Provide accurate handoff information  
– Monitor initial climb for conflicts  
– Ensure no conflict with other departing or circuit traffic  

Where required, the controller shall:

– Issue initial climb instructions  
– Apply heading or altitude restrictions as coordinated  

Example:

```text
GFA123, contact Bahrain Radar 119.7.
```

---

## 5.2.5 Transfer of Control

Transfer of control shall be completed in a timely and coordinated manner.

The Tower controller must:

– Transfer aircraft before leaving the area of responsibility  
– Ensure correct frequency is issued  
– Avoid frequency congestion where possible  

The receiving unit assumes responsibility once:

– Two-way communication is established, or  
– As defined by local procedures  

---

## 5.2.6 Coordination Failures

If coordination cannot be established:

– Do not issue takeoff clearance if release is required  
– Hold aircraft at the holding point  
– Attempt to re-establish communication  

If already airborne:

– Apply standard separation  
– Use conservative control measures  
– Protect the aircraft from conflicting traffic  

---

## 5.2.7 Key Principles

Effective coordination is based on:

– Clarity  
– Accuracy  
– Timeliness  
– Standard phraseology  

Controllers must:

– Coordinate early  
– Avoid assumptions  
– Confirm critical information  
– Maintain situational awareness across units  

Poor coordination is a common cause of:

– Loss of separation  
– Runway conflicts  
– Inefficient traffic flow
