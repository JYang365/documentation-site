# 5.3 Arrival Coordination

Arrival coordination ensures safe and efficient integration of inbound aircraft into the aerodrome environment.

---

## 5.3.1 General Principles

Arrival coordination shall be conducted to:

– Maintain safe separation  
– Ensure an orderly landing sequence  
– Minimise delays and unnecessary vectoring  
– Support efficient runway utilisation  

The Tower controller must maintain awareness of all inbound traffic and expected sequencing.

---

## 5.3.2 When Coordination is Required

Coordination is required when:

– An Approach or Radar controller is online  
– Aircraft are transferred on final approach  
– Special handling is required (e.g. priority, emergency)  
– Sequencing adjustments are necessary  

If no higher unit is online, Tower assumes full responsibility for sequencing and separation.

---

## 5.3.3 Transfer from Approach

Aircraft are normally transferred from Approach:

– Established on final approach  
– At a defined distance from touchdown  
– With sequencing already assigned  

The Tower controller must:

– Acknowledge the inbound aircraft  
– Confirm runway and landing sequence  
– Monitor spacing and runway occupancy  

Example:

```text
APP → TWR: GFA456, established ILS runway 27.
TWR: GFA456, continue approach runway 27.
```

---

## 5.3.4 Sequencing Arrivals

The Tower controller must ensure:

– Adequate spacing between successive arrivals  
– Wake turbulence separation is applied  
– Runway occupancy time allows safe landing  

Methods of sequencing include:

– Speed control (if applicable)  
– Extending final approach (via Approach)  
– Adjusting circuit traffic  
– Issuing go-arounds when required  

---

## 5.3.5 Landing Order Management

Landing order shall be:

– Clearly established early  
– Communicated to pilots where necessary  
– Adjusted only when safe and required  

The controller should:

– Avoid last-minute changes  
– Prevent compression of spacing  
– Anticipate conflicts before they occur  

Example:

```text
GFA456, number two, follow traffic on short final.
```

---

## 5.3.6 Runway Occupancy Considerations

Before issuing landing clearance, ensure:

– The runway is clear or will be clear in time  
– Preceding aircraft has vacated or is vacating  
– No crossing traffic is authorised  

Controllers must monitor:

– Exit taxiways  
– Vacating speed of aircraft  
– Potential delays on runway  

---

## 5.3.7 Missed Approach / Go-Around Coordination

If an arrival cannot land safely:

– Issue go-around immediately  
– Inform Approach if required  
– Re-sequence the aircraft  

The controller must:

– Protect the missed approach path  
– Ensure separation from other traffic  
– Update sequencing accordingly  

Example:

```text
GFA456, go around, I say again go around.
```

---

## 5.3.8 Coordination with Adjacent Units

Tower must coordinate with Approach when:

– Changing landing sequence significantly  
– Issuing multiple go-arounds  
– Runway becomes unavailable  
– Traffic demand exceeds capacity  

Effective coordination ensures:

– Smooth traffic flow  
– Reduced controller workload  
– Increased safety margins  

---

## 5.3.9 Key Principles

Arrival coordination must prioritise:

– Safety over efficiency  
– Early planning over reactive control  
– Clear communication over assumption  

Controllers must:

– Maintain continuous situational awareness  
– Monitor final approach at all times  
– Anticipate runway availability  
– Act early to prevent conflicts  

Failure in arrival coordination may result in:

– Loss of separation  
– Go-arounds  
– Runway incursions  
– Increased workload and reduced safety
