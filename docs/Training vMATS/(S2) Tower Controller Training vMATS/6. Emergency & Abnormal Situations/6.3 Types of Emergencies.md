## 6.3 Types of Emergencies

Aircraft emergencies may arise from a variety of technical, operational, or medical issues. The Tower controller must recognise the type of emergency and respond appropriately.

---

### 6.3.1 Engine Failure

Engine failures may occur during any phase of flight but are most critical during takeoff and initial climb.

The controller shall:

- Prioritise the aircraft immediately  
- Ensure the runway is available for landing  
- Stop all departures if required  
- Provide wind and runway information without delay  

Example:

GFA123, MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY, engine failure after departure.  
GFA123, roger MAYDAY, runway 27 cleared to land, wind 270 degrees 8 knots.

---

### 6.3.2 Radio Communication Failure (R/T Failure)

Loss of communication may be indicated by:

- No response to calls  
- Incorrect or incomplete readbacks  
- SSR code 7600  

The controller shall:

- Continue to transmit instructions assuming the aircraft can receive  
- Observe the aircraft for compliance  
- Apply standard radio failure procedures  
- Ensure separation from other traffic  

Example:

GFA456, if you read, squawk IDENT.

---

### 6.3.3 Medical Emergencies

Medical situations onboard may require priority handling.

The controller shall:

- Provide priority landing  
- Minimise delays  
- Coordinate with emergency services if required  

Example:

GFA789, PAN PAN, medical emergency onboard, request priority landing.  
GFA789, roger PAN PAN, runway 27 cleared to land.

---

### 6.3.4 Aircraft Systems Failure

Failures may include:

- Hydraulic failure  
- Electrical failure  
- Navigation system issues  
- Pressurisation problems  

The controller shall:

- Provide priority handling as required  
- Allow extended approaches if necessary  
- Avoid complex instructions  

---

### 6.3.5 Unlawful Interference (Hijack)

Indicated by SSR code 7500 or pilot report.

The controller shall:

- Acknowledge discreetly  
- Avoid actions that may increase risk  
- Follow local security procedures  
- Continue to provide normal service unless instructed otherwise  

---

### 6.3.6 Fuel Emergencies

Aircraft may report:

- Minimum fuel (advisory)  
- Fuel MAYDAY (emergency)  

The controller shall:

- Monitor closely when minimum fuel is reported  
- Provide priority when fuel emergency is declared  
- Avoid delays  

Example:

GFA321, MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY, fuel emergency.  
GFA321, roger MAYDAY, runway 27 cleared to land.

---

### 6.3.7 Unstable Approach

Indicators include:

- Excessive speed or descent rate  
- Incorrect configuration  
- Poor alignment  

The controller shall:

- Monitor closely  
- Be prepared to issue a go-around  
- Maintain separation  

---

### 6.3.8 Runway Incidents

Includes:

- Aircraft unable to vacate runway  
- Vehicle or obstruction on runway  
- Disabled aircraft  

The controller shall:

- Stop all arrivals and departures  
- Issue go-arounds if required  
- Coordinate with ground and emergency services  

---

### 6.3.9 Bird Strike / Environmental Hazards

Includes:

- Bird strikes  
- Severe weather  
- FOD hazards  

The controller shall:

- Provide updated information to aircraft  
- Allow flexibility in routing or approach  
- Monitor affected aircraft  

---

### Additional Guidance

- Always prioritise the nature and severity of the emergency  
- Avoid overloading the pilot with information  
- Keep instructions simple and relevant  
- Continuously reassess the situation  

---
