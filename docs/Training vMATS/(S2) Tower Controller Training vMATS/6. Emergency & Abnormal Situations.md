# 6. Emergency & Abnormal Situations

Emergency and abnormal situations require immediate, decisive action to maintain safety. Controllers must prioritise affected aircraft while maintaining overall situational awareness.

---

## 6.1 General Principles

In all emergency or abnormal situations, the controller must:

– Prioritise safety above all other considerations  
– Provide maximum assistance to the aircraft  
– Maintain clear and concise communication  
– Avoid overloading the frequency  
– Coordinate with all relevant units  

The controller shall remain calm, structured, and proactive at all times.

---

## 6.2 Identification of Emergencies

An emergency may be identified by:

– Pilot declaration (MAYDAY / PAN PAN)  
– Abnormal pilot behaviour or requests  
– Loss of communication  
– Unexpected aircraft performance or deviation  

Emergency phases:

– **INCERFA**: Uncertainty phase  
– **ALERFA**: Alert phase  
– **DETRESFA**: Distress phase  

Controllers must treat all potential emergencies seriously until confirmed otherwise.

---

## 6.3 Controller Priorities

When handling an emergency, priorities are:

1. Separate aircraft  
2. Establish communication  
3. Provide assistance  
4. Coordinate with other units  
5. Maintain overall traffic awareness  

Other traffic shall be delayed, re-sequenced, or instructed as required.

---

## 6.4 MAYDAY (Distress)

A MAYDAY indicates grave and imminent danger.

Controller actions:

– Acknowledge immediately  
– Clear the runway and surrounding airspace  
– Provide direct routing if required  
– Suspend non-essential operations  
– Coordinate with Approach and emergency services  

Example:

```text
GFA789, roger MAYDAY, runway 27, cleared to land, wind 270 degrees 8 knots.
```

---

## 6.5 PAN PAN (Urgency)

A PAN PAN indicates urgency but not immediate danger.

Controller actions:

– Provide priority handling  
– Maintain separation from other traffic  
– Monitor the situation closely  
– Be prepared to escalate if required  

Example:

```text
GFA456, roger PAN PAN, continue approach runway 27, you are number one.
```

---

## 6.6 Radio Failure (R/T Failure)

If an aircraft experiences radio failure:

– Observe standard procedures (e.g. squawk 7600)  
– Maintain predictable traffic flow  
– Protect the aircraft’s expected path  
– Avoid conflicting clearances  

The controller must:

– Monitor for light signals (if applicable)  
– Continue to provide separation  
– Anticipate pilot actions based on procedures  

---

## 6.7 Aircraft Emergency on the Runway

If an aircraft becomes disabled on the runway:

– Immediately close the runway  
– Instruct all aircraft to go around or hold position  
– Coordinate emergency response  
– Inform all affected traffic  

Example:

```text
All stations, runway 27 blocked, go around, acknowledge.
```

---

## 6.8 Loss of Separation

If separation is lost or about to be lost:

– Take immediate corrective action  
– Issue avoiding instructions  
– Use simple, clear phraseology  

Actions may include:

– Go-around  
– Stop departure  
– Issue heading or altitude instructions (if applicable)  

Example:

```text
GFA123, go around immediately.
```

---

## 6.9 Unstable Approach

If an approach becomes unsafe:

– Issue go-around without delay  
– Do not wait for pilot request  
– Maintain separation with other traffic  

Indicators include:

– Excessive speed  
– Incorrect alignment  
– Late descent profile  

---

## 6.10 Low Fuel / Fuel Emergency

If an aircraft reports low fuel:

– Provide priority handling  
– Avoid delays  
– Coordinate for immediate landing  

If minimum fuel or MAYDAY FUEL is declared:

– Treat as priority/emergency  
– Ensure immediate landing capability  

---

## 6.11 Weather-Related Situations

Adverse weather may include:

– Low visibility  
– Strong winds or windshear  
– Thunderstorms  

Controller actions:

– Increase spacing  
– Provide updated weather information  
– Be prepared for go-arounds or diversions  

---

## 6.12 Bird Strike / Foreign Object Damage

If an aircraft reports a bird strike:

– Assess aircraft condition  
– Provide priority landing if required  
– Inspect runway if necessary  

---

## 6.13 Security Incidents

Security situations may include:

– Hijacking  
– Unlawful interference  
– Suspicious behaviour  

Controller actions:

– Follow local procedures  
– Maintain discretion  
– Avoid alerting other aircraft unnecessarily  
– Coordinate with appropriate authorities  

---

## 6.14 Coordination During Emergencies

During emergencies, the controller must coordinate with:

– Approach / Radar  
– Adjacent aerodromes  
– Emergency services  

Information to pass:

– Callsign  
– Nature of emergency  
– Runway in use  
– Intentions  

---

## 6.15 Communication Techniques

Effective communication is critical:

– Use standard phraseology  
– Speak clearly and calmly  
– Avoid unnecessary transmissions  
– Confirm critical instructions  

Do not overload the pilot with information.

---

## 6.16 Post-Incident Actions

After the situation is resolved:

– Resume normal operations gradually  
– Ensure runway is safe and available  
– Document the event if required  
– Review actions taken  

---

## 6.17 Key Principles

Emergency handling is based on:

– Immediate action  
– Clear communication  
– Strong situational awareness  
– Effective coordination  

Controllers must:

– Act decisively  
– Prioritise correctly  
– Maintain control of the situation  

Failure to manage emergencies effectively may result in:

– Loss of separation  
– Runway conflicts  
– Increased risk to aircraft and passengers
