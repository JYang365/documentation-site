# 3.3 Common Controller Errors

During Tower operations, certain errors are frequently observed in developing controllers. Recognising and correcting these early is essential to maintaining safe and effective control.

---

## 3.3.1 Loss of Situational Awareness

Loss of situational awareness occurs when the controller no longer has an accurate mental picture of traffic.

Common causes:

- Focusing on a single aircraft  
- High workload without prioritisation  
- Poor scanning of the aerodrome environment  

Indicators:

- Forgetting aircraft positions  
- Issuing conflicting instructions  
- Delayed reactions to developing situations  

Mitigation:

- Continuously scan all traffic  
- Keep a mental or visual sequence  
- Reduce workload if required  

---

## 3.3.2 Late Decision Making

Late decisions often result in unsafe or inefficient outcomes.

Examples:

- Issuing a go-around too late  
- Delaying sequencing until aircraft are too close  
- Reacting instead of anticipating  

Mitigation:

- Make decisions early  
- Plan ahead for each aircraft  
- Anticipate conflicts before they occur  

---

## 3.3.3 Incorrect Runway Separation

Failure to maintain runway separation is a critical error.

Examples:

- Clearing an aircraft to land with traffic still on the runway  
- Issuing takeoff clearance with traffic on short final  
- Allowing runway crossings without protection  

Mitigation:

- Always verify runway status before issuing clearance  
- Monitor final approach continuously  
- Use conservative judgement when unsure  

---

## 3.3.4 Poor Phraseology

Incorrect or unclear phraseology can lead to misunderstandings.

Examples:

```text
"Yeah you can go now"
"Taxi whenever ready"
"Line up quickly"
```
---

## 3.3.5 Over-Controlling

Over-controlling occurs when the controller issues excessive or unnecessary instructions.

Examples:

- Giving unnecessary instructions in the circuit  
- Overloading pilots with information  
- Intervening when not required  

Mitigation:

- Issue only necessary instructions  
- Allow pilots to operate normally where appropriate  
- Focus on safety-critical control  

---

## 3.3.6 Poor Workload Management

Failure to manage workload can quickly lead to unsafe situations.

Examples:

- Accepting too many aircraft into the circuit  
- Continuing departures during high arrival demand  
- Not slowing the operation when required  

Mitigation:

- Reduce traffic complexity early  
- Delay departures when necessary  
- Prioritise safety over traffic flow  

---

## 3.3.7 Failure to Act

Failure to act is often more dangerous than taking the wrong action.

Examples:

- Not issuing a go-around when required  
- Hesitating during developing conflicts  
- Ignoring unsafe spacing  

Mitigation:

- Act decisively when safety is at risk  
- Use standard solutions (go-around, delay, orbit)  
- Avoid hesitation  

---

## 3.3.8 Key Principle

Most controller errors result from poor situational awareness, late decisions, or failure to act. Recognising these early and applying standard corrective actions is essential to safe Tower control.
